Group 1
Students with Diagnosed ADHD
ADHD is a neurological, neurodevelopmental disorder that is largely genetic, with heritability estimated at 70–80%. It is characterised by persistent inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that are present across multiple settings from before age 12, and are lifelong. In Australia, 6–10% of adolescents are estimated to have ADHD, with the inattentive presentation being the most common.

These students need structured supports not because their environment shaped their attention, but because their neurological profile means that unstructured, high-demand tasks — like open-ended exam questions — generate executive function overload.
Australian ADHD Clinical Practice Guideline (2026) — AADPA
Group 2
Students with Technology-Related Attention Impairment
A growing number of students show ADHD-like symptoms — impaired attention, reduced working memory, and diminished executive functioning — as a result of excessive social media and digital media use. Critically, it is not total screen time but media multitasking and short-video consumption that drives this effect.

A major longitudinal study tracked 2,587 teenagers aged 15–16 without pre-existing ADHD symptoms over 24 months. Those using 14 high-frequency digital media activities showed a 10.5% rate of developing ADHD symptoms, compared to only 4.6% in those with no high-frequency media use — more than doubling the rate.
Ra et al., JAMA (2018) — PubMed
Uncapher et al. (2021) — Media multitasking driving factor — PMC
Important Caveat The relationship is bidirectional — students with ADHD are also more likely to use digital media heavily. Research cannot yet cleanly establish what proportion of classroom attention difficulties are caused by vs. correlated with heavy media use.
Key Finding
Same Classroom Strategies Benefit Both Groups
Whether attention difficulties are neurologically based (ADHD) or environmentally conditioned (technology habits), the research evidence supports the same classroom response: structured, low-distraction, high-engagement design.

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) research is explicit on this: when instruction is designed for students at the edges of the population — including those with ADHD — all students benefit. Strategic seating arrangements, chunked tasks, visual cues, immediate feedback and explicit teaching support not only students with ADHD diagnoses but the entire class.
NC State / DELTA (2024) — UDL and neurodivergent students — delta.ncsu.edu
Tandfonline (2025) — Micro-level classroom practices benefit whole class — Link
The Problem
Why Exam Writing is Hard for ADHD Students
Working Memory, Planning, and Writing Quality
A systematic review found that attention, short-term memory, working memory, and executive function deficits are the primary drivers of high-order writing difficulties in students with ADHD. Writing quality and the writing process — particularly planning and editing — are more challenging for ADHD students than writing productivity.
Cheng et al. (2022) — Systematic review, ADHD and high-order writing — ScienceDirect
Similar Length, Worse Quality
A controlled study of 84 students with ADHD and 135 matched controls found that ADHD students wrote texts of similar length to peers but with significantly poorer structure, coherence, and ideation. An ADHD diagnosis explained 6.7% of variance in writing quality after controlling for IQ and age — with half of that effect directly attributable to working memory and sustained attention deficits.

Critically, ADHD students spent significantly less time on planning — using writing processes that were very basic and fixed — which directly caused the omission of fundamental components in their responses.
Rodríguez et al. (2015) — ADHD and writing quality — PDF
Rodríguez et al. (2015) — Planning deficits — ERIC

Feature by Feature
Research Behind Each App Feature
Feature 1
C-U-B-E Question Deconstruction — Cognitive Load & Task Initiation
The single biggest ADHD barrier to exam writing isn't knowledge but knowing how to start a question.

Research shows that individuals with ADHD exhibit hyposensitivity of dopamine neurons in response to rewarding stimuli. The importance of a task does not trigger enough neurotransmitter activity to initiate action.

ADHD brains require 2–3 times more dopamine stimulation to initiate tasks compared to neurotypical brains. Specific, concrete "if-then" plans significantly improve follow-through for people with ADHD — specificity reduces the mental energy needed to figure out where to start.

The C→U→B→E sequence is essentially a built-in "if-then" starter protocol. Instead of facing a blank page with an open-ended question, students face the concrete first step: circle the directive verb. This is a single, unambiguous, low-cognitive-demand action that triggers the initiation response.
NSW DoE Cognitive Load Theory Practice Guide (2017) — NSW DoE
AERO / Pearson (2023–2024) — Chunking and cognitive load — Pearson
OT4ADHD (2024) — Task initiation and dopamine — OT4ADHD
Source Note The "2–3 times more dopamine stimulation" finding originates from Harvard Medical School and neuroscience research, but the saskadhd.com source is a practitioner website, not a peer-reviewed journal.
Feature 2
Working Memory Offloading via Physical Annotation
ADHD is consistently associated with moderate weaknesses in working memory — the system that temporarily stores and manipulates information while performing complex tasks. Meta-analysis confirms working memory as a core deficit in ADHD.

The four-step CUBE annotation process externalises the planning process that working memory normally must hold. Instead of keeping "what does this question want, what are the keywords, what are the constraints, what evidence do I need" all active simultaneously, each element is physically marked on the page — offloading that cognitive burden entirely.
Willcutt et al. (2005) meta-analysis — ADHD and working memory — via PMC
Feature 3
Planning / Annotation Tab — Structured Pre-Writing
Students with ADHD spend very little time planning and use writing processes that are basic and fixed, which directly causes the omission of fundamental components in their responses.

A study using the Self-Regulated Strategy Development model with high school students with ADHD showed a marked improvement in the number of essay elements, length, and holistic quality of essays after explicit planning strategy instruction. Crucially, planning time also increased, and students maintained gains at three-week follow-up.

The Planning Tab forces this phase to occur as a concrete, separate, visible, scored step — making it rewarding rather than abstract.
Rodríguez et al. (2015) — ADHD planning deficits — ERIC
Johnson & Reid (2011) — SRSD writing strategy for ADHD high school students — ResearchGate
Feature 4
Immediate AI Feedback — The Dopamine Fix
Neuroimaging research found that individuals with ADHD show decreased activity of the dopamine reward pathway, including reduced activation of the ventral striatum in response to both immediate and delayed rewards. This translates directly into problems engaging in activities that are not inherently rewarding.

Traditional marking — where a student submits work and receives feedback days or weeks later — is almost entirely incompatible with ADHD neurology. The delayed reward provides no dopamine signal at the moment of effort.

For all learners, a 2024 systematic review (37 empirical investigations) found that AI can enhance customisation, deliver immediate feedback, and increase student engagement. Formative assessment with timely, specific feedback improves conceptual understanding, reduces anxiety, and enhances academic performance.

The CUBE app's ability to provide the student with immediate feedback on their written response provides the student with the immediate reward of a mark and how to improve their performance.
Volkow et al. (2010) — Dopamine reward pathway in ADHD — PMC
Frontiers in Education (2025) — AI feedback systematic review — Frontiers
Feature 5
Gamification — XP, Bands, Streaks, Leaderboard
A 2025 randomised controlled trial provided empirical evidence that gamified learning tools significantly enhance both attention performance and academic achievement in children with ADHD. Interactive engagement, instant feedback, and reward mechanisms support improvements in attention and enhance motivation.

A 2025 systematic review of 21 studies (2021–2024) found gamification positively influences student motivation and often enhances academic performance in primary and secondary education. The most effective game elements were points, badges, leaderboards, progress tracking, and real-time feedback.

The CUBE app's point system, band progression, and leaderboard provide multiple, layered reward signals. For an ADHD student who might not be intrinsically motivated by "getting better at HSC writing," the extrinsic reward structure provides the dopamine trigger that sustains engagement through the full submission process.
Dai et al. (2025) — RCT: gamified tools and ADHD — Frontiers
Nurhayati et al. (2025) — Systematic review, gamification in school education — AL-ISHLAH
Nuance A 2023 meta-analysis found gamification effect sizes are larger in higher education (g = 0.869) than secondary school (g = 0.014). This suggests that at secondary level, design matters more than gamification alone — the CUBE app's focus on personal improvement (XP progression, band movement) alongside competition is well-aligned with what works at this level.
Feature 6
Model Answers — The Worked Example Effect
The worked example effect is described by cognitive load theorists as their most important finding. Students given model answers to study before writing perform significantly better than those who attempt the problem themselves without models. Asking students to write extended answers without worked examples "unnecessarily adds problem-solving search to the interacting elements, thus imposing an extraneous cognitive load."

NSW DoE's Cognitive Load Theory Practice Guide explicitly endorses worked examples as a primary evidence-based strategy: students learn new content more effectively when given worked examples than when required to solve problems independently.

The CUBE APP shows a band-specific model answer for the exact question just attempted — with the same syllabus keywords, the same directive — which explicitly displays a clear modelled response.
NSW DoE Cognitive Load Theory Practice Guide (2017) — NSW DoE
Feature 7
Syllabus Keyword Identification (U Step) — Explicit Academic Vocabulary
Explicit instruction in academic vocabulary provides a language foundation for understanding texts across all subject areas. As discipline-specific words become automatic, students can shift their mental effort toward content, making vocabulary instruction an essential scaffold for disciplinary literacy.

The CUBE app's U step explicitly trains students to identify and use syllabus-specific vocabulary. Research on academic language development in secondary students confirms that the use of academic word-list vocabulary is a measurable discriminator between basic and more advanced student writing.
Savvas Insights (2026) — Academic vocabulary and disciplinary literacy — Savvas
Kim & Graham (2022) — Academic language in secondary writing — PMC
Feature 8
Teacher Analytics Dashboard — Data-Informed Teaching
The AERO Evidence-Based Practices in School Settings for Student Wellbeing report (2025) assigns its highest confidence rating — Level 4: Very High — to strategies that involve teachers monitoring and responding to student data. Data-informed teaching is consistently identified as among the highest-impact educational practices.

The CUBE APP Analytics Dashboard provides class-level data on band distributions, CUBE accuracy, literacy focus areas, and submission patterns. This enables teachers to actively monitor student outcomes, identify areas of weakness in student responses and adjust teaching practice accordingly.
AERO (2025) — Evidence-Based Practices in School Settings — edresearch.edu.au
